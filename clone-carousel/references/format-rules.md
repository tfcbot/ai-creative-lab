# Format rules — how to read a reference carousel

A carousel format is the recurring grammar of a post. Cover does X. Middle slides hold Y. Closer does Z. Caption obeys pattern P. If you can't write down those four lines after looking at the slides, you haven't extracted the format yet.

## The three slide roles

Every well-designed carousel has three roles, regardless of length:

### Cover (slide 1)
The slide that has to stop the swipe. Always carries the headline. Often carries a brand mark. Almost always type-heavy.

What to extract:
- **Headline content** — the actual words
- **Headline placement** — top-third / center / lower-third, layered on the photo or sitting in negative space
- **Headline style** — font weight, color, accent treatment (single italic word, single colored word, outlined keyword)
- **Brand mark** — wordmark vs. logo tile vs. nothing, position, size relative to headline
- **Photographic subject** — who/what is in the photo, the scene, the camera POV

### Body (slides 2 through N-1)
The slides that earn the swipe. Hold the same visual rule across all of them. This rule is what makes seven different photographs read as one carousel.

What to extract — list every invariant you can spot:
- **Subject pattern** — same person every slide? same category of subject (always a flatlay, always a selfie)?
- **Camera POV** — selfie / mirror / overhead / third-person / mixed?
- **Lighting era** — golden hour / window light / studio / mixed?
- **Color palette** — warm / cool / desaturated / saturated?
- **Text pattern** — text on every body slide / no text on any body slide / text only on slides 2 and 5?
- **Composition rhythm** — full body / close-up / mixed in a deliberate sequence?
- **Era / aesthetic** — 90s film grain / 2010s Instagram / 2026 hyperreal AI?

A strong format has 4–6 body invariants. A weak format has 1–2 (the carousel feels random) or 8+ (the carousel feels constrained and stilted).

### Closer (slide N)
The slide that converts attention into action. Always has a CTA. Often has type. Sometimes has the strongest hook of the whole carousel.

What to extract:
- **CTA mechanic** — comment a keyword, save the post, follow for more, DM the creator
- **CTA visual treatment** — pill-shape with imperative inside, outlined keyword box within a longer phrase, just bold all-caps text, no special treatment
- **Photographic subject** — selfie / scene / object that signals the brand?

## The caption pattern

The caption is half the format. Carousel + caption are a unit. Read the caption like a copywriter:

- **Hook (line 1)** — a question / a promise / a contrarian claim / a "this is X" reveal? How many words?
- **Body (next 2–4 lines)** — proof / explanation / numbered list / story?
- **CTA (last 1–2 lines)** — match the closer slide's CTA mechanic? Different ask?
- **Hashtags** — count, mix of niche-specific and broad-category, position (inline or appended)

## What "format" is NOT

- **Vibe.** "It's aesthetic" is not a format.
- **Single slide.** A great cover doesn't make a format. The format is what holds across slides.
- **Brand identity.** The brand is the variable; the format is the constant.

## How to know your format extraction is good enough to clone

Read the format.md you wrote. Hand it to someone who hasn't seen the reference. Could they sketch the carousel from your description? Could they generate slide specs from it without questions? If yes, you're done. If no, look at the slides again.

## How to handle confused references

Some carousels mix multiple sub-formats in one post (slides 1–3 are listicle, slides 4–6 are a vibe-board, slide 7 is a CTA). These don't clone cleanly. Two options:

1. **Pick the strongest sub-format** and clone only it (e.g. clone the listicle, drop the vibe-board, keep the CTA — write a cleaner post)
2. **Pick a different reference.** A confused reference produces a confused clone.

Tell the user which path you picked and why.
